Is Inflation always Bad?

Inflation is often intepreted as a plague - but is it truly the barrier to our prosperity, or merely a misunderstood saviour? Well, this question truly is a torture. However, there exists no absolute correct answer to this question. Instead debates has been ongoing due to inflation's different effects based on its rate, causes, and whether its anticipated or not

Lets start off with the very basics; what is inflation? Well in easy words, its how much more expensive goods become upon certain change in the country's economy. In more technical terms, inflation is the sustained increase in the general price level within an economy over time.

Commonly perceived negatively due to rising prices, it is important that we understand that inflation holds great responcibility in encouraging investments and incentivising costumer spending in the contemporary time.

Despite severe downsides inflation can bring, the ultimate aim of inflation is to adjust real wages and to encourage investments.

Upon expecting price rise, people would be more willing to engage in Investments due to future expectations of higher prices and thus people are more likely to purchase sooner than later.

Furthermore, inflations act as an alternate to wage cuts, reducing employees' real wages while remaining at a constant nominal wage.

At elevated levels, inflation pose significant threats to economic stability, and potentially undermining consumer confidence. In extreme cases such as the Zimbabwe or Weimar Republic, monetary value collapsed so drastically that basic goods becamse unaffordable.

As stated by Keynes, Inflation is less severe than deflation and should not be feared indiscriminately. but instead taken as a neccessary, a factor that controls (while effectly utilized) a country's economical health.

Does Economic Growth always lead to Improved Living Standards?

R

ising GDPs always makes headlines,

but can a booming economy truly be called successful if its citizens continue to struggle?

Economic growth is determined based on the increased real GDP over time, within a given country. And usually we consider the well-being of citizens including material satisfaction (income, consumption) and the non material aspects.

While economic growth often correlates with improved living standards, the true impact is determined by how the gain is distributed.

Image

Considering higher economic growth within a country means that there is higher GDP, it would create higher income and consumption rate within the area, as a result enhancing access to goods and services alongside healthcare, education, and infrastructure. This Material improvement in daily life is most often the direct impact felt by the average person. Furthermore, growth tends to stimulate investments and spending rates, creating jobs across multi sectors. With more employment opportunities, povertiy rates decilne and more people gain a steady source of income.

Additionally, economic growth increases governemtns' revenue through higher tax receipts - this enables the state to invest more in education healthcare, and improving citizens' living standards. To a great extent, this results in higher satisfaction and happiness due to the enhanced lifestyle.

Image

A very good example would be China's post-2000 economic thrive, which pulled a stunning 800 million people out of povertiy a helped create a substancial middle class. In such cases, economic growth acts as a powerful engine that boosts both the individual prosperity and national growth.

Despite its potential benefits, economic Growth does not always guarantee a corresponding rise in living standards, especially when the gains are extremely unevenly distributed between social clases. In many economies, growth has disproportionately benefited the wealthy, leaving large segments of the population behind. For instance, India has experienced rapid GDP expansion in recent years, yet income inequality remains severe, with millions still living in poverty and lacking access to basic services. Such example tells us that despite increasing economy within a country may occur, great excludable and rivalrous policies may still lead to the majority living under unidealistic conditions.

Image

While economic growth is often interpreted as a sign of national progress, it does not directly correlate to individual living standards.

Growth can bring tangible improvements in income, employment, and public services when it is inclusive and well-managed. However, if the gains are unequally distributed or achieved through unsustainable means, the result may be rising inequality.
